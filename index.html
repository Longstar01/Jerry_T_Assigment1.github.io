IT Project

Eksplorador One

“Eksplorador one” roguelike space-adventure program based on command-lines. A real-time strategy/simulation game in which one player explores and navigates a procedurally generated world. The player is a drone fleet operator in the fringes of the galaxy exploring and scavenging various derelict and forgotten ships scattered around the outer reaches of civilised space. The objective of the games is to scavenge, explore, expand the operators fleet, and maybe investigate why there are so many derelicts left in the fringes of humanity. The controls of the game will be a mix of general controls of a typical real-time strategy and the player using command lines to direct and assign tasks to the operator's drone fleet.

The motivation stems from my deep interest and fascination of strategy and role-playing adventure games. An exciting component of the game will be the implementation of using a command console to interact and control the drones used in the game. There are very few instances of bringing back the old command prompts once prominent in older computer systems but was eventually replaced by windows. The belief is that this would help promote interest from people into programming.

The initial primary objective of the game is to pay off the enormous debt garnered by a former partner of the drone operator. To pay off the massive debt owed to the Grant Foundation: they send you off in an old exploratory carrier with a few workers, combat, and control drones to salvage the fleets of derelict ships found in the frontier. The frontier will be a procedurally generated stretch of space consisting of several types of star systems the operator can explore.

At the start of the game will consist of the operator and their fleet jumping out of Waypoint, a massive frontier space station at the edge of civilised space. Here is also where the operator sells salvage, performs extensive maintenance on both the carrier and advanced drones, gets newer and better gear for both the ship and the drones and lastly pays the Grant foundation a part of the operator's debt or else be hunted down by the Grant Foundations Hunter-Killer debt collection squadron. The systems outside the Waypoint are all procedurally generated mixed with random events. These events may be useful (for example, encountering a random debris field contain valuable scavenge floating in space), neutral (another scavenging ship passing by informing the operator of something such as an event occurring in a nearby system), or bad (a pirate ambush, solar flare, sudden malfunction in the carrier, etc.). The primary aspect of the gameplay will be exploration and scavenging derelict ships in the fringes of the frontier. The later is performed by both selecting and inputting commands on drones to salvage a ship. This process is not instant or a method of click and forget the operator has to do several steps to salvage a vessel such as:


                          > sending drones inside to deactivate any systems still operating,
                          > shutting of volatile engineering systems and automated defences
                          > killing unknown and random entities in the ship (pirate scavengers, alien lifeforms automated defence drones.
                          > managing and deciding which cargo is more valuable to sell at Waypoint etc.

The efficient management of this aspect of the game is critical in fulfilling the primary objective of the game. More exploration may lead to scripted events with in-depth lore about the game and massive payout or losses to the operator if it goes poorly.

Another significant aspect of the gameplay is the technological progression of the operator's ship and a fleet of drones. Early on the operator only has a small number of drones equipped with a small amount of modifications and abilities. It is up to the operator to slowly upgrade his/her little fleet to be able to take in a broader variety of tasks as this will help in the end goal of paying off the operator's debt as he/she can take on larger contracts dealing with massive vessels like Capital-ships and large intergalactic cargo containers.

Lastly, the player may be forced in several combat-related events. For example, a pirate ship ambushes the operator out of the blue and is significantly better equipped; the operator may be forced to use worker drones to take damage to protect the carrier. Such actions may result in the loss of the drone and all modifications but the continued operation of the carrier.
